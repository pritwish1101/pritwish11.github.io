I traced my academic lineage through my PhD advisor Nils Engelsen, his advisor Mark Kasevich, and so on — all the way back to a self-taught American mathematician at the turn of the 19th century. The chain runs almost entirely through Harvard and early American physics, with a detour through Berkeley and Stanford for the modern atomic-physics generations.

Each entry below was verified against the Mathematics Genealogy Project, the Harvard physics PhD thesis records (1873–1953), National Academies biographical memoirs, and Nobel Foundation sources. Listed from present (me) to past.

---

### Pritwish Agarwal
**PhD:** Chalmers University of Technology, Sweden (expected 2028)
**Thesis (provisional):** *Single Strontium-88 Atoms Coupled to Nanophotonic Cavities*

### Nils J. Engelsen
**PhD:** Stanford University (2016)
**Thesis:** *Quantum Metrology Using Large Ensembles of Entangled Atoms*

### Mark Kasevich
**PhD in Applied Physics:** Stanford University (1992)
**Thesis:** *Atom Interferometry in an Atomic Fountain*

### Steven Chu &nbsp;<small>★ Nobel Prize in Physics, 1997 (shared with Claude Cohen-Tannoudji and William D. Phillips)</small>
**PhD:** University of California, Berkeley (1976)
**Thesis:** *Observation of the Forbidden Magnetic Dipole Transition Doublet 6 ²P<sub>1/2</sub> → 7 ²P<sub>1/2</sub> in Atomic Thallium*

### Eugene D. Commins
**PhD:** Columbia University (1958)
**Thesis:** *Hyperfine Structure of the Metastable State of Singly Ionized Helium-3*

> Commins's own advisor at Columbia was Polykarp Kusch — the same Kusch listed immediately below — making this a particularly tight piece of academic lineage.

### Polykarp Kusch &nbsp;<small>★ Nobel Prize in Physics, 1955 (shared with Willis Eugene Lamb)</small>
**PhD:** University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign (1936)
**Thesis:** *The Molecular Spectrum of Caesium and Rubidium*

### Francis Wheeler Loomis
**PhD:** Harvard University (1917)
**Thesis:** *The Heat of Vaporization of Mercury*

### Harvey N. Davis
**PhD:** Harvard University (1906)
**Thesis:** *I. A PQ Plane for Thermodynamic Cyclic Analysis; II. The Longitudinal Vibrations of a Rubbed String*

### John Trowbridge
**Doctor of Science (S.D.):** Harvard University (1873)
**Thesis:** *I. A New Form of Galvanometer; II. On the Electromotive Actions of Liquids Separated by Membranes; III. On the Electrical Condition of Gas Flames; IV. Ohm's Law Considered from a Geometrical Point of View; V. Induced Currents and Derived Circuits*

### Joseph Lovering
**A.B.:** Harvard University (1833)
**Thesis:** *The Application of the Principle of Fluxions to the Solution of Different Problems*

> Harvard did not grant physics PhDs until decades later; an A.B. with a thesis was the terminal degree of its time. Lovering would later become the first director of Harvard's Jefferson Physical Laboratory.

### Benjamin Peirce
**A.M.:** Harvard University (1829)

No formal dissertation in the modern sense. Peirce was the first American mathematician of international research reputation, later Perkins Professor of Astronomy and Mathematics at Harvard, and the academic adviser to Joseph Lovering.

### Nathaniel Bowditch
**Self-taught.**

Famous work: *The New American Practical Navigator* (1802) — still in continuous publication, and carried on every commissioned U.S. Navy vessel.
